One Sentence

Inflation is not "prices rising"; inflation is durable balance-sheet expansion through collateral creation.

Everything else in the papers is a consequence of that sentence: wages are transfers, reserves are swaps, QE is usually collateral removal, high asset prices are evidence of money scarcity, fiscal issuance can crowd in risk, and Volcker's myth is the story of mistaking market rates for Fed control.

Primitive Vocabulary

Money

Not M1, M2, reserves, or cash in isolation. Money is the broad set of liabilities attached to assets, including observable and unobservable balance sheets.

Collateral

The asset side of money: promises to future payment that can be pledged, sold, financed, or reused. Collateral is the monetary base that matters.

Inflation

Sustained price acceleration financed by new money. Price spikes without new collateral are reallocation and demand destruction.

Velocity

Income / assets. A growth, productivity, or real-rate concept. It can raise nominal activity, but without continuous new collateral it becomes fragile.

Balance Sheet Space

The true production frontier of money. If banks, shadow banks, or the public sector cannot or will not expand balance sheets, inflationary potential falls.

Risk

Risk-taking is money creation. One person's borrowing is another person's asset, collateral, income, or equity.

Model Families

1. Grease vs Friction Inflation

Grease means new collateral lets higher prices keep clearing without destroying demand. Friction means prices rise because supply/growth capacity falls, so the price increase itself tightens the system.

Friction shock → Q falls → some promises fail → collateral falls → demand destroyed → transitory price spike Grease shock → assets rise → liabilities rise → demand financed → more assets → persistent inflation

2. Stock / Flow Balance

Financial asset values are the stock; income, spending, yields, and cash flows are the flow. Without new asset creation, pushing up stock pulls from flow.

3. Three Balance-Sheet Forest

Inflation analysis fails when it watches only one tree. The supply of money is the sum of public issuance, private observable credit, and private unobservable/shadow/offshore credit.

Money creation = Public balance sheet + Private observable balance sheets + Private unobservable balance sheets

4. Return Parity / Insurance

Safe assets are insurance for risky activity. When policymakers make insurance expensive by buying it, risk-taking does not automatically rise; balance sheet space gets consumed by insurance needs.

Cheap insurance → less capital needed for hedging → more room for risky lending Expensive insurance → more capital needed for hedging → less room for risky lending

Market-Reading Heuristics

High Safe Yields

Usually not tight money. They can indicate abundant asset creation, high income/assets, and real-economy liquidity.

Low Safe Yields

Often signal scarcity of trusted collateral, high demand for liquidity, poor real-economy liquidity, and deflationary pressure.

Curve Steepening

Can be reflationary when it improves bank profitability and signals more balance-sheet capacity.

Curve Inversion

Signals shortage of longer-duration collateral relative to short-term funding costs; the expansion engine is impaired.

Expensive Equities

Not automatically strong growth. They can be bond-like scarcity assets, especially large-cap/quality/US names in a low-yield regime.

Dollar Strength

Can signal global dollar collateral shortage, not US growth exceptionalism. Watch offshore/shadow balance-sheet stress.

Stress Tests

Necessary Conditions For Durable Inflation

  • New asset-liability pairs are being created repeatedly, not one-shot.
  • Collateral is sufficiently useful, accessible, liquid, and creditworthy.
  • Balance-sheet creators are earning enough spread/return to keep expanding.
  • Safe assets/insurance are available enough to support true risky lending.
  • Policy is adding equity/risk, not only bidding up existing assets.

False Positives

  • Wage growth without credit or dissaving.
  • Commodity/supply shocks that impair future collateral.
  • Reserves growth without private collateral growth.
  • Asset-price inflation without cash-flow growth.
  • Velocity spikes financed by finite savings drawdowns.

Open Tensions Worth Preserving

  • Government money creation: the papers say governments do not create money like banks, but public deficits clearly create collateral and private surpluses. The clean formulation is: governments create collateral directly and money indirectly through bank/dealer absorption and fiscal spending.
  • Rates as policy vs conditions: the same rate move means different things depending on whether markets set it as a condition signal or the Fed hijacks it as a policy signal. This distinction is load-bearing and should always be explicit.
  • QE taxonomy: QE is too broad. The correct taxonomy is desired-asset QE, distressed-asset purchases, par-lending/equity support, maturity swaps, and true fiscal issuance.
  • Unobservable money: the system depends on offshore, shadow, FX-swap, repo, and derivative balance sheets that are hard to measure. Market prices become primary evidence, not secondary evidence.
