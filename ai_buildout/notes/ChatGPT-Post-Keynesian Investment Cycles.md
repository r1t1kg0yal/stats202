I’ll map the mechanism you’re describing to the Post-Keynesian/Minsky/Kalecki framework, then separate what is Moore/Harvey from what is more Kaleckian-Goodwin/Minskyan.

The theory you’re reaching for is best described as a **Post-Keynesian real-financial investment cycle**, combining:

**Keynes/Harvey:** investment is the unstable driver of the business cycle. Harvey’s Post-Keynesian business-cycle account says capitalist economies generate cycles endogenously, with the rise and fall of investment spending at the center of expansions and recessions. ([ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/228936921_The_US_Business_Cycle_Since_1950_A_Post_Keynesian_Explanation))

**Moore/horizontalism:** credit money is endogenous. Banks do not wait for prior saving; loans create deposits, and the central bank accommodates reserve demand at its target rate. So an investment boom can finance itself through credit expansion, at least until balance sheets, collateral, confidence, or policy conditions change. ([Levy Economics Institute of Bard College](https://www.levyinstitute.org/pubs/wp_512.pdf))

**Minsky:** investment is financed by promises to pay out of future cash flows. During good times, firms and banks extrapolate stability, take on more leverage, and move from hedge finance toward speculative/Ponzi positions. Minsky explicitly frames the economy as a “veil of money” system where funds flow to firms based on expected future profits, and repayment must later be validated by realized profits. ([Levy Economics Institute of Bard College](https://www.levyinstitute.org/pubs/wp74.pdf))

**Kalecki:** aggregate profits are strongly tied to capitalist expenditure, especially investment. In the simplified Kalecki story, capitalists “get what they spend”: investment spending creates aggregate profits ex post. So the problem is not simply that “investment cannot raise aggregate nominal profits.” It can. The problem is that the **stock of capital, debt commitments, and required profit claims can grow faster than realized nominal cash flows**.

A clean version of the mechanism is:

> **Inflation / rising demand → higher markups and profits → optimistic expectations → credit-financed investment boom → rising capacity and productivity → competitive or demand-side pressure on prices → lower nominal revenue growth / disinflation / deflation → profit squeeze relative to debt and capital stock → investment collapse → recession.**

So your intuition is close, but I would phrase the key contradiction slightly differently:

**Investment creates profits as a flow, but it also creates capacity, debt, and future required cash-flow commitments as stocks.** The boom becomes unstable when the expected nominal profit stream needed to validate the enlarged capital/debt structure fails to materialize.

In symbols, the tension is something like:

$$
\text{Fragility rises when } \frac{\text{debt service + required return on capital}}{\text{nominal cash flow / profits}} \uparrow
$$

Even if real productivity improves, that can be destabilizing if it lowers prices or reduces nominal sales growth. A productivity boom can raise real output while weakening nominal profit validation, especially when debts are fixed in nominal terms. That is the Fisher-Minsky debt-deflation channel layered onto a Post-Keynesian investment cycle.

The theory is therefore **not** exactly “real growth causes the bubble to pop because nominal profits cannot rise in aggregate.” It is more:

**Credit-financed investment booms raise real capacity and financial commitments. If nominal demand and prices do not keep growing fast enough to validate those commitments, the same investment boom that produced expansion produces overcapacity, disinflation, profit disappointment, and financial fragility.**

That is very Minskyan, but with a stronger **Harvey/Kalecki/Moore** flavor:

1. **Moore:** credit accommodates the boom.
2. **Kalecki:** investment generates profits while the boom lasts.
3. **Harvey/Keynes:** investment is volatile because expectations and the marginal efficiency of capital reverse endogenously.
4. **Minsky:** the financial structure built during the boom becomes fragile and then amplifies the downturn.

A compact label for what you mean would be:

**“A Kalecki-Minsky investment cycle with endogenous money and a productivity/price-profit squeeze.”**

Or, more informally:

**“A Post-Keynesian endogenous investment boom that undermines its own nominal profit validation.”**

From this Post-Keynesian perspective, **real growth can reduce the return on capital when it expands the physical capital stock faster than the nominal profit flow needed to validate it**.

The key distinction is:

$$
r_K \approx \frac{\Pi}{K}
$$

where:

$$
\Pi = \text{nominal profits}, \qquad K = \text{value of capital stock}
$$

Real growth often means more machines, data centers, factories, software systems, capacity, and productivity. That raises **K**. But the return on capital only rises if nominal profits **Π** rise at least as fast.

If real growth is driven by investment and productivity, it can reduce returns through several channels.

First, **capacity rises relative to demand**. Each firm invests because it expects high demand. But when many firms do this together, aggregate capacity expands. Unless final nominal demand expands equally fast, firms face lower utilization or price pressure. More capital is chasing the same, or only modestly larger, nominal spending stream.

Second, **productivity growth lowers unit costs, which can become lower prices**. At the firm level, a productivity improvement can raise margins. But at the sector level, competition often passes cost reductions into prices. Real output rises, but nominal revenue per unit falls. That means society can get more real stuff, while capitalists earn a lower nominal return on the enlarged capital stock.

For example:

| Situation | Capital stock | Nominal profits | Return on capital |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Before boom | 100 | 20 | 20% |
| After real investment boom | 200 | 25 | 12.5% |
| After overcapacity/price pressure | 200 | 15 | 7.5% |

Real capacity doubled, but profits did not. The return falls.

Third, **investment creates future fixed costs**. New capital brings depreciation, debt service, lease payments, maintenance, power contracts, and required returns. Even if productivity improves, the new capital has to be financially validated. If nominal cash flows disappoint, the realized return on that capital falls.

Fourth, **what looks profitable for one firm may be self-defeating in aggregate**. One company can invest, lower costs, gain market share, and raise its profit rate. But if all companies invest, the aggregate result can be excess capacity, lower prices, and compressed margins. This is the Post-Keynesian/Kaleckian fallacy-of-composition point.

Fifth, **real growth can shift income away from profits**. If productivity gains are captured by workers through wages, by consumers through lower prices, or by downstream firms through cheaper inputs, then the real surplus may rise while the profit share does not. In that case, capital productivity improves physically, but financial returns weaken.

So the PK answer is:

**Real growth does not automatically reduce the return on capital. It reduces it when the growth is capital-deepening, competitive, debt-financed, and disinflationary, so the capital stock and financial claims rise faster than aggregate nominal profits.**

The AI/data-center version is:

More GPUs and data centers raise real compute capacity. That can lower the price of inference. If cheaper inference creates enough new demand, profits can rise. But if prices fall faster than usage expands, then the industry has more capital earning less revenue per dollar of capital. Real AI output rises, but the return on AI capital falls.

Compactly:

$$
\text{More real capacity} \not\Rightarrow \text{higher return}
$$

because:

$$
\text{return on capital} =
\frac{\text{nominal profit flow}}{\text{capital stock to be validated}}
$$

If the denominator grows faster than the numerator, real growth lowers the return.

For AI, there are several reasons consumer surplus could be huge while profits stay thin.

**AI can be socially transformative and financially overbuilt at the same time.**

This is the same difference as:

$$

$$

A society can get an enormous increase in use value from AI agents without generating a proportional increase in exchange value for the owners of AI capital.

```text

This is plausible. | Channel | How nominal profits can still rise |

| Data/control rents | Agents become gateways to commerce and software |

**Who captures the surplus, and can they capture enough of it in nominal cash flow to validate the capital stock?**

If not, the real boom becomes a profit-rate problem.

The strongest version of your argument is:

**AI agents may produce a large unmeasured or under-monetized real surplus. Because the surplus is competed away, bundled, or captured by users rather than infrastructure owners, the nominal profit flow remains too small relative to AI capex. Thus the economy can experience genuine AI-driven real growth while the AI data-center investment boom still ends in a Minskyan bust.**

That is not a contradiction. It is almost exactly what a PK/Minsky lens would predict in a capital-intensive technological boom: **real transformation plus financial overaccumulation.**

If usage explodes but revenue and gross profit rise much less, your story is right. If usage explodes and firms capture a large share through subscriptions, enterprise contracts, cloud, ads, or labor-cost savings, the boom can be validated.
