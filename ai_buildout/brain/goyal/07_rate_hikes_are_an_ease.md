## The rates ⇄ NGDP black hole

```
        ┌─────────┐        pull          ┌─────────┐
        │  RATES  │ ◄──────────────────► │  NGDP   │
        └─────────┘   (arb between the    └─────────┘
              ▲         real & financial         ▲
              │         economies)               │
   "rates DESCRIBE conditions"        "conditions DESCRIBE rates"
              └─────────────── SAME THING ────────┘

   ⇒ a rate is ALWAYS an interpretation of conditions
   ⇒ the Fed cannot "hijack" rates so they mean the opposite
   ⇒ ∴ the correlation of Δshort-rates to Δcredit is ALWAYS POSITIVE
   test: "Great Depression with no Fed" — rates would have gone to 0%. Easing?
```

## Two readings of one rate (the bridge to Volcker)

```
   A RATE IS RISING. What does it mean?
        │
        ├── as CONDITIONS (market-set):  borrowing ↑ ⇒ conditions LOOSENING   ◄ this view
        │
        └── as POLICY (Fed "hijacks"):   Fed hikes  ⇒ policy   TIGHTENING      ◄ consensus

   policy runs AGAINST conditions ⇒ these are OPPOSITE interpretations of the
   identical rate signature. Read rates as conditions ⇒ high rate = strength.
```

## "Rates are the rate we pay each other"

```
   a rate HIKE = the Fed decreeing the price of EVERYTHING is higher
   = inflationary IFF balance sheets can EXECUTE the adjustment
        depends on SAVINGS / ability to adjust (BTFP made execution easy)
   same logic as ch.01–02: higher prices are sustainable iff there's money to pay them.
```

## Diversification = the only free lunch

```
   the Fed adds NO aggregate equity — cutting rates PUNISHES one party, REWARDS
   another (pure redistribution). Its one real power: provide DIVERSIFICATION,
   which requires a HIGH risk-free yield (a safety net to move out the risk curve).
   "There's no free lunch except diversification."
```

## Bonds as insurance — the worked example

```
   $1000 portfolio; want a $100 payout in a recession; 10y bonds as insurance.

   CASE 1: yields 6% → 3% in recession    CASE 2: yields 2% → 0% in recession
     ΔP = -Δy × dur = +30; +avg yld 4.5     ΔP = +20; +avg yld 1
     payout per $100 bond = $34.5            payout per $100 bond = $21
     need 100/34.5 → $290 of bonds           need 100/21  → $476 of bonds
     ⇒ $710 FREE for risk                    ⇒ only $524 free for risk
                                             ($186 SWALLOWED by dear insurance)

   ⇒ LOW yields crowd OUT risk (insurance eats the budget; remaining players go
     under-insured). HIGH yields crowd IN risk. Cheap insurance ⇒ more risk-taking.
   ⇒ also: higher yields = higher VELOCITY (each dollar of insurance does more).
```

## Rate hikes redistribute — they don't simply deflate

```
   effect on EFFECTIVE-book A-L spread when rates rise:
        Households   WIDEN        Businesses    WIDEN
        Chartered banks NARROW    Shadow banks  NARROW   (marginal book widens)

   ⇒ ~half the curves steepen, ~half narrow. The AGGREGATE A-L curve is always
     FLAT (every steep curve has an inverted counterparty). Rate hikes change WHO
     wins (housing/startups lose; the riskiest, credit-starved players win because
     banks can now extend credit to them). Change it too FAST and everyone loses.

   ⇒ "Risk Charity": higher rates REWARD everyone short assets (future income >
     present savings = everyone without assets), PUNISH asset holders. PROGRESSIVE.
   ⇒ "use it or lose it": high rates ⇒ money can't sit idle ⇒ velocity ↑.
```

## Lead/lag: why a PAUSE precedes recession

```
   a PAUSE stops the stimulative act of hiking → the CUT then exacerbates.
   "It's when the Fed says 'we're done hiking' that causes the recession."
   Fed + conditions self-reinforce BOTH directions; a THIRD (exogenous) thing
   causes the turns.

   TRADING:  use RATES to predict rates (short rates = most forward-looking input
   to conditions). Using conditions to trade rates = playing catch-up.
   2022–23: priced cuts / inverted curve = a TAIL HEDGE bet that "something breaks"
   — reasonable, but post-cyclically there were no landmines left to step on.
```

## The Fed is cyclical; the fiscal authority is the counter-cyclical force

```
   the Fed isn't necessary NOT because the cycle self-corrects (Andy's reason),
   but because the Fed acts CYCLICALLY (its model is risk-ADDITIVE = inflationary).
   A counter-cyclical force IS still needed — the FISCAL authority:
        rates high ⇒ risk supply high ⇒ fiscal should do AUSTERITY (reduce risk)
        rates low  ⇒ risk supply low  ⇒ fiscal should EXPAND
```
