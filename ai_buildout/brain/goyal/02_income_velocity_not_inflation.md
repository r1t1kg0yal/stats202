# 02 · Income & Velocity aren't Inflation

> CORE CLAIM: There is no inflationary potential in the *circulation* of money,
> only in its *production*. The wage-price spiral is a myth. Velocity is a growth
> concept, not an inflation concept. Dissaving can only ever produce transitory
> inflation, because savings are finite.

## Supply of money vs circulation of money

```
   CREATION (inflationary)                 CIRCULATION (not inflationary)
   ─────────────────────────              ─────────────────────────────────
        COMMERCIAL BANK                    BUSINESS            HOUSEHOLD
   ┌──────────┬───────────┐          ┌────────┬─────┐    ┌──────────┬─────┐
   │ +Asset   │ +Liability│          │ -Cash  │     │ ─► │ +Income  │     │
   └──────────┴───────────┘          └────────┴─────┘    └──────────┴─────┘
   balance sheet GROWS                balance sheets only RECOMPOSE
   (new money)                        (a wage = the firm spends less elsewhere)
```

A wage hike is just a price hike on labor. With no new money, every buyer who
pays a higher price must buy less of everything else — the market-clearing price
of the rest must fall. **Wages are one cross-section of prices, not an input to
the aggregate.** (A chart of wages vs CPI looks like a chart of milk vs CPI.)

## How can I increase my spending? (only one branch is inflationary money)

```
                     I increase my spending by...
        ┌──────────────────┬──────────────────────┬─────────────────────────┐
        ▼                  ▼                      ▼                          ▼
    BORROWING          DISSAVING            SPEND LESS ELSEWHERE        RAISE MY INCOME
   new money         activates idle         pure reallocation         someone else now
   created           money (0→1 spend)      (NOT inflationary)         spends less, unless
   INFLATIONARY      INFLATIONARY*          ──────────────────         THEY borrowed/dissaved
                     *but transitory                                   → recurse to left
```

Dissaving = "borrowing from your future self." It raises velocity from 0 to 1
for a dollar — but it is a one-shot. Sustained inflation needs *continuous*
fresh dissaving or money creation.

## The 4-player dissaving economy (the Bob-Elliott debate, in numbers)

2 households, 2 businesses; everyone starts at $100; service price = wage = $20.
H1 gets a $10 stimulus check; one revolution raises price and wage $20 → $30:

```
                 HH1   HH2   Bus1  Bus2
   Start         100   100   100   100
   H1 +$10 stim  110   100   100   100
   B2 P 20→30    [80]  100   100   130     ← H1 dissaved to 80
   H1 wage 20→30 110   100   [70]  130
   B1 P 20→30     110  [70]  100   130     ← H2 dissaved to 70
   H2 wage 20→30  110   100   100   100
   ──────────────────────────────────────
   RESULT: price 20→30, wage 20→30, every saving FLOOR fell 80→70
```

```
        ┌──── BALANCING LOOP (why it self-corrects) ─────┐
        │ spend more via dissaving → savings ↓ toward     │
        │ a personal FLOOR → players hit floors → dissave │
        │ capacity ↓ → spending ↓ → prices stop rising    │
        └─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
   If players refuse to go below $70, prices CANNOT keep rising. And saving is
   mean-reverting: the natural response to a windfall is to SAVE it, not dissave
   more. A velocity-led inflation hits more and more speed-bumps until it stops.
```

> Could the babysitter co-op price hit 100 scrip with a fixed scrip supply?
> Not practically — each dollar would have to be "claimed" by ever more players.
> This is functionally **collateral rehypothecation**: more claims on the same
> base = more activity but more fragility. And inflation is strength, not fragility.

## Velocity = real interest rates

```
   from MV = PY  ⇒  Velocity = Income / Assets

   Bond Yield       = Coupons  / Price
   Equity Earn-Yld  = Earnings / Price
                       └──────────────►  Income / Assets

   ∴  VELOCITY  =  YIELDS  =  REAL INTEREST RATES
```

```
   raising rates  ──►  devalues assets vs their cash flows
                  ──►  raises INCOME relative to WEALTH (flow vs stock)
                  ──►  pulls money from the future to the present
                  ──►  fewer dollars sit dormant ⇒ higher velocity ⇒ higher growth
```

This is the seed of the great inversion (ch.07): higher rates → higher velocity →
stimulative. The 2022-23 hikes raised *real rates* (velocity) without raising
*breakevens* (money) — growth held up, inflation fell, because money handed
itself around faster. The Fed's balance sheet is irrelevant to this; M1/M2 don't
capture true money (Greenspan: "we cannot locate money").
